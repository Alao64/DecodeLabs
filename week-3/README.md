🛒 Online Retail Customer Segmentation — Week 3: Business Intelligence & RFM Analysis


Decode Labs | Week 3 Project



Overview

This notebook applies business intelligence techniques to an online retail dataset, transforming raw transactional data into actionable customer insights. The core objective is to build a customer-level feature set using RFM (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) analysis, enriched with additional behavioural metrics, followed by exploratory data analysis, feature scaling, and dimensionality reduction via PCA.

This work bridges the gap between raw data and business strategy — turning transaction logs into a lens for understanding customer behaviour.


Dataset

Source: online_retail.csv

A transactional dataset from a UK-based online retailer, covering purchases between December 2010 and December 2011.

Key features include:

FeatureDescriptionInvoiceUnique invoice number for each transactionStockCodeProduct/item codeDescriptionProduct nameQuantityNumber of units purchased per transactionInvoiceDateDate and time of transactionPriceUnit price of the productCustomer IDUnique customer identifierCountryCountry of the customer

Dataset shape: 541,910 rows × 8 columns


Notebook Breakdown

1. Data Cleaning


Loaded the dataset and inspected its structure (shape, dtypes)
Checked for missing values — 1,454 missing Description values and 135,080 missing Customer ID values were identified
Dropped rows with missing Customer ID values, as they are essential for customer-level analysis
Identified and removed negative quantity rows, which represent returns or cancellations, reducing the dataset to 397,925 valid rows
Ran descriptive statistics to understand the distribution of key numerical features


2. Feature Engineering — Customer-Level Aggregation

A new customer-level dataframe was constructed by aggregating transaction data to derive six behavioural metrics per customer:

FeatureDescriptionBusiness InterpretationRecencyDays since the customer's last purchase (relative to one day after the dataset's last date)Lower recency = more recently active customerFrequencyNumber of unique invoices (orders) per customerHigher frequency = more loyal/engaged customercustomer_revenueTotal revenue generated by the customer (Quantity × Price)Higher revenue = more valuable customerAvgOrderValueAverage revenue per order (customer_revenue / Frequency)Measures spending per transactionUniqueProductsNumber of distinct products purchased by the customerBroader product engagement = diverse purchasing behaviourTotalQuantityTotal number of units purchased across all ordersReflects overall purchase volume

This results in a condensed customer dataframe with 4,339 unique customers and 6 features.

3. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Descriptive statistics revealed significant variability across all customer features, with heavy right-skewed distributions — indicative of a small cohort of high-value customers driving disproportionate revenue.

Key visualisations:


Histograms — Distribution of all six customer features, highlighting skewness in recency, frequency, and revenue
Correlation heatmap — Identified strong positive correlations between Frequency, TotalQuantity, and customer_revenue, suggesting that order frequency is a key driver of total revenue
Frequency vs Customer Revenue (scatter plot) — Confirmed the positive relationship between how often a customer purchases and how much they spend
Recency vs Customer Revenue (scatter plot) — Explored whether recently active customers tend to generate more revenue


4. Feature Scaling


Applied StandardScaler to normalise all six customer features
Ensures that features with larger numeric ranges (e.g., TotalQuantity) do not disproportionately influence downstream analysis


5. Dimensionality Reduction — PCA


Fitted a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) model on the scaled data to assess variance explained by each component
Computed cumulative explained variance to guide decisions on how many components to retain for clustering or further modelling
